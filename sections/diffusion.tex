Performance directly correlates with step count:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Under-sampling ($T=8, 16$):} High formatting failure rates. Because $k_t$ is large, the model must simultaneously commit to many low-confidence tokens, turning early steps into blind guesses. This disproportionately breaks shorter sequences (Level 1).
    \item \textbf{Optimal ($T=32$):} Accuracy peaks at 80.0\%. Unmasking $k_t=2$ tokens per step provides sufficient granularity to correct early mistakes, eliminating formatting failures entirely.
    \item \textbf{Saturation ($T=64$):} No further improvements observed.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Qualitative Examples}
When $T \ge 32$, errors on complex Level 3 problems are mostly due to poor state-tracking rather than syntax errors. For example: \\
\texttt{glorp(flarn(snib(12, 4), 4))} $\rightarrow$ Expected: 12 | Got: 8. \\
The model computes the inner operation ($12-4=8$) but fails to propagate the result through the outer addition.
